Speaker Notes

If the problem is this big, why hasn't anyone solved it? Three reasons.

First, language. Every credible safety tool is English-first. Bangladesh's scams happen in Bangla and Banglish — romanized Bangla typed in English letters — and global models simply don't read it well.

Second, they're not always-on. Even Google's and Norton's newer scam tools mostly need you to paste a message in after you're already suspicious. Scams work precisely on people who aren't suspicious yet.

Third, and most important — the wrong mental model. The few "chat monitoring" products that exist are parental-control apps for children. An adult woman doesn't want a guardian watching her. She wants to protect herself. That reframing — self-protection, not surveillance — is our entire design philosophy, and it's why we're also privacy- and policy-safe.

How It Works

Surokkha AI5 / 12
Speaker Notes

Under the hood there are three layers. Layer one is a hard-rules engine — fast regular-expression patterns that catch the unmistakable scams: any request for an OTP or PIN, lottery wins, "send a fee to claim your prize." This runs instantly, offline, and never misses the obvious — even if the AI is slow or unavailable.

Layer two is the AI classifier. In this demo it's Google Gemini; in production it becomes an on-device BanglaBERT model so nothing leaves the phone. This is what understands subtle, well-written, paraphrased scams across Bangla, Banglish and English.

Layer three explains the verdict — a risk score and the top two or three reasons, in Bangla. That transparency is what turns a skeptical user into someone who trusts and keeps the app.

And our long-term moat is layer four: a Truecaller-style community reputation network for scam senders.
